entry no. 004

the audio is purged the moment the words land

24 July 2026 · the shyn daybook

The going approach to meeting memory is a bot. It joins your call wearing a name like “Notetaker,” records everyone, ships the audio to a cloud, and mails back a summary written by someone else's computer. It works. It also means the most candid hour of your day now exists as a file you don't control, on infrastructure you've never seen, under a retention policy you clicked past.

shyn's version stays in the room. When a meeting starts, shyn notices — call detection runs on-device, and it verifies actual voice before committing to a recording, so a YouTube video doesn't become a “meeting.” It records both sides locally, with a pulsing red dot in your menu bar the whole time. Never silent, never sneaky: the people who built this dislike being recorded without knowing too.

When the call ends, Whisper — running on your Mac's own silicon, no API in sight — turns the audio into a speaker-labeled transcript, and the transcript is filed into the encrypted register like any other memory. Then the part we consider the actual feature: the audio is deleted, immediately. Not retained for quality purposes. Not kept until a sweep gets around to it. Words in, bytes gone.

Why destroy the recording?

Because the recording and the memory are different things. What you want on Thursday is “what did we decide about the pricing page” — that lives in text, searchable in milliseconds alongside everything else you've read and written. What the audio holds is everything else: tone, tempers, the sound of a colleague's kitchen. Keeping it is all liability and no recall. A transcript can answer a question; a recording can only be replayed against someone.

The asymmetry runs through shyn's retention design. Screen captures age out on a rolling window — ambient context loses value fast. Meeting transcripts are kept indefinitely by default — decisions are the highest-value memories you have. And shyn forget overrides everything: by document, by source, by time range, honestly deleted.

The fine print, as always

Meetings need the Microphone and System Audio permissions, which macOS will ask you for in its own words. Detection is tuned for calls, and it is pre-alpha software: edge cases exist, and sustained non-speech audio can still occasionally look meeting-like. When in doubt, the red dot tells the truth — and shyn meeting stop and cancel are one command away.
